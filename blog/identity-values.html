Identity, Values, and Strengths

9 August 2025

I am the Results Seeker

They are dominant, stimulated by challenge, decisive and direct. They can be blunt, stubborn, and can lose sight of the big-picture. Thay can be insensitive to other people's needs. They are natural achievers.

1. Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision.

As I’ve grown older and taken on more responsibility—particularly as a parent—I’ve found myself making more ethical decisions, often shaped by a desire to model compassion and fairness for my children. One example that stands out was during my daughter’s fifth birthday, a milestone that holds particular cultural significance in New Zealand. In NZ, turning five is more than just a birthday—it marks a transition from kindergarten to primary school and is often celebrated with enthusiasm and pride.

At the time, we were living in Australia, where school starts more routinely at the beginning of the year. Wanting to give my daughter a celebration that reflected the importance of this moment, I organised a party and invited all the children in her class—including one child who had developed a reputation for picking on others. It didn’t sit right with me to exclude anyone, especially a child, no matter their behaviour. I believed then—and still do—that exclusion only reinforces the kind of isolation that often contributes to difficult behaviour in the first place.

After the party, some of the other parents stopped speaking to me. They clearly disapproved of my decision to include that child. But from an ethical standpoint, I felt strongly that inclusion, empathy, and fairness were values I wanted to uphold, especially in front of my daughter. Excluding a five-year-old from a celebration because of their behaviour felt wrong.

Looking back, I stand by my decision. It was more important to me that no child felt deliberately left out, and that my daughter saw an example of kindness, even when it's not popular. Ethics aren’t always comfortable—but they should always reflect the kind of world we’re trying to create.

2. Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.

I’ve never felt entirely comfortable with the word “culture.” It’s often tied to visible markers—skin colour, language, accents, traditions. I don’t fit neatly into those categories. I’m not visibly “other.” I don’t speak with a foreign accent outside of New Zealand. When a form asks me to tick “Pākehā,” or someone remarks on my Italian surname, I’m reminded again that my cultural identity feels vague, even fragmented.

What I do have is a strong sense of values—perhaps stronger than a defined identity. Those values have been shaped largely by the people around me, particularly my mother and grandmother, whose influence continues to echo in my life—for better or worse. They taught me the importance of independence, resilience, and showing up for others even when it’s hard. I’ve carried those values into how I work, parent, and live.

I value time. I value relationships. I value honesty. But I often find myself questioning the labels that come with identity. Am I where I “should” be at 43? According to whom? I’m a wife—often referred to as “Greg’s wife.” I’m a mother—“Nick and Jacquie’s mum.” I manage soccer, water polo, and synchronised swimming schedules like a full-time job. I run a business—but that, too, is complicated. I’ve ghostwritten for so long that sometimes I question when I’m speaking as myself, or on behalf of someone else. Even that has shaped how I understand authenticity.

So while I may not point to a specific cultural heritage with a clear name or flag, I am deeply influenced by the roles I hold and the people I care for. My culture, if I have one, is stitched together from lived experience, inherited strength, and the quiet act of choosing what matters, every day.

3. Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

My top strengths are curiosity, love of learning, creativity, hope, and perspective. Together, they create a powerful foundation for any learning journey—especially one that requires independent thinking and self-direction, like flipped learning.

Curiosity drives me to ask questions, explore ideas, and dig deeper. It means I rarely accept surface-level explanations and am motivated to understand how and why things work. Paired with a love of learning, this ensures that I stay engaged, even when things get challenging. I genuinely enjoy the process of learning—not just the outcomes—and that makes a big difference when motivation dips.

Creativity helps me approach problems in unique ways. I often find myself making unexpected connections between ideas or reframing problems so they make more sense to me. This is especially helpful when navigating complex or unfamiliar material, or when traditional learning methods don’t quite resonate.

Hope gives me the resilience to keep going. I believe that effort leads to progress, even when things feel difficult. It allows me to view setbacks as temporary and to stay focused on what I can do next. And perspective allows me to zoom out when needed—to see the bigger picture, to reflect on what matters, and to not get lost in the noise of comparison or pressure.

Together, these strengths will support me not just in acquiring new knowledge, but in doing so with confidence, curiosity, and adaptability. They keep me open-minded, engaged, and optimistic—which is exactly the mindset I want to bring to my learning journey.

4. Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?

Some of my lesser strengths include gratitude, perseverance, forgiveness, and love. While these might not traditionally be seen as “limitations,” they do suggest areas where I may need to be more intentional in how I approach both learning and career development.

For instance, lower perseverance can mean I’m more likely to lose momentum when I don’t see immediate results, especially in areas that don’t feel personally rewarding. I also recognise that if I’m not actively practicing gratitude or forgiveness—towards myself or others—it can impact how I process feedback or deal with setbacks. These are important areas for growth, especially in environments where progress isn’t always linear.

That said, this journey isn’t about starting from scratch—it’s a side hustle, not my sole career path. In fact, it’s part of my third career, which gives me a different kind of advantage. I bring with me a broad range of life and professional experiences, along with the maturity to see things in context. I also know that “career development” doesn’t have to follow a single path or timeline. With roughly 22 years of potential professional contribution still ahead, I see this as a flexible opportunity to learn something new, build additional skills, and remain adaptable.

Overall, while I’m aware of the limitations, I think my realistic mindset—and the fact that I’m not putting pressure on this to be everything—means I can approach it with a healthy balance of ambition and perspective.

5. Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

Earlier in my career, my default response to tension or resistance when working with others was to simply walk away. If the conversation was difficult or draining, I’d disengage—physically or emotionally. But as a business owner, I’ve had to evolve. These days, I give myself space: I don’t answer the phone if I’m not ready, I vent privately (often in colourful language), and I allow myself the time to rewrite and reframe an email before sending it. These small rituals give me the space to respond thoughtfully rather than reactively.

One practical strategy that has consistently worked for me is aligning people with their strengths—especially when collaboration is tense. For example, if someone is being difficult but excels in design, I’ll frame the task around that: “This really needs your design eye—look at this mess, it’s calling for your magic.” Is it a little manipulative? Maybe. Is it effective? Absolutely. It gets the work done, improves buy-in, and defuses friction.

I trace this strategy back to high school, where a teacher introduced us to Dr. Edward de Bono’s Six Thinking Hats method. It changed how I approach group dynamics. The Hats provide a common language for collaboration, reduce confrontation by focusing on one mode of thinking at a time, and encourage diversity of thought. It taught me that reframing tension as a thinking style—not a personality clash—can create room for productivity.

Looking back, I can see that walking away was really about avoiding conflict. Today, I aim to reduce tension through redirection, reframing, and giving space—both to myself and others. Moving forward, I’d like to be more deliberate about applying tools like De Bono’s Six Action Shoes, especially the Pink Slippers (human caring) and Purple Boots (appropriate authority), depending on what the situation calls for. These frameworks offer more than conflict avoidance—they offer a structured way to lead with empathy, purpose, and strategy.
