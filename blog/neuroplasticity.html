Neuroplasticity

Emotional intelligence (EI) is generally understood as the ability to recognize, understand, and manage one’s own emotions, as well as to perceive and influence the emotions of others, encompassing both self-awareness and social awareness skills (Mayer et al., 2003; Goleman, 2001). It is considered distinct from cognitive intelligence (IQ) and personality traits, with research demonstrating that EI can predict important outcomes such as workplace performance, leadership effectiveness, and interpersonal relationships, even after controlling for IQ and personality factors (Cherniss et al., 2006). In workplace contexts, EI contributes to building effective communication, collaboration, and conflict management skills, thereby enhancing organisational performance and employee well-being (Mental Health America, n.d.). Moreover, EI can be cultivated through structured programs—often referred to as social and emotional learning (SEL)—which have been shown to improve social skills, academic achievement, and mental health outcomes in educational settings (Cherniss et al., 2006; Collaborative for Academic, Social, and Emotional Learning, 2005).

Growth Mindset

Emotional intelligence (EQ) differs from intelligence quotient (IQ) in that EQ refers to the ability to recognise, understand, and manage one’s own emotions, as well as perceive and influence the emotions of others, supporting empathy, self-regulation, and effective relationship building (BetterUp, n.d.; Dovetail, n.d.). In contrast, IQ measures cognitive abilities such as logical reasoning, problem-solving, and analytical thinking, which are less focused on emotional and social contexts (BetterUp, n.d.; Dovetail, n.d.). While IQ can contribute to academic and technical performance, EQ plays a critical role in communication, conflict resolution, and overall interpersonal effectiveness (Dev Academy, n.d.; BetterUp, n.d.).

The importance of Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset on learning

Emotional intelligence (EQ) is important because it enables individuals to recognise, understand, and manage their own emotions as well as the emotions of others, which is essential for effective communication, collaboration, and relationship-building (Wikipedia; MHA National). In contrast to IQ, which measures cognitive ability, EQ is linked to empathy, adaptability, and self-regulation, skills that are crucial for navigating social and professional environments (Dovetail; BetterUp). In the workplace, higher EQ supports leadership, teamwork, and conflict resolution, while also helping individuals cope with stress and change, ultimately contributing to both personal wellbeing and organisational success (MHA National; Student Handbook).
