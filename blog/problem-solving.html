Problem Solving

23 September 2025

Testing, Problem Solving, Psuedocoding and more!

1. Unblocking a simple problem:

I was blocked on what seemed like a simple problem. The instructions I had been given did not align with the resources provided, which created confusion and uncertainty about how to proceed. To resolve this, I looked through the supplied materials, compared them with the resources I knew should apply to the project, and then checked back in with the project manager for clarification. At first I felt quite confused, but once the issue was confirmed I felt validated that the challenge was not due to my misunderstanding, but rather an inconsistency in the handover. From this experience I learned that sometimes obstacles are not about my own ability or understanding, but instead about things that happened prior to me receiving the file, and it is important to recognise when the problem lies in process or communication rather than in myself.

2. Elegantly solving a problem:

I was faced with a problem that needed an elegant solution: camel case had to be installed, although at the time I did not even know what camel case was. To work through the issue, I used console logging to test my assumptions and turned to ChatGPT for guidance. Throughout the process I felt both lost and very smart at the same time — lost because I was learning something completely new, yet smart because I was able to apply the tools at my disposal effectively to reach a solution. From this experience I not only solved the immediate issue but also learned the difference between camel case and snake case, which gave me new knowledge I can apply in future coding tasks.

3. Problem-solving techniques/processes:

  • Pseudocode

    I usually write pseudocode in my head, but through this course I have found it useful to make it part of my actual process. Formalising it on paper or screen helps clarify my logic and gives me a stronger foundation before I start coding.

  • Trying something

    Experimenting has always been my natural way of solving problems, and in fact, it is what led me to join this course in the first place. I feel confident in this approach as it encourages exploration, risk-taking, and learning through trial and error.

  • Rubber Ducky Method

    I really enjoy the rubber ducky method and find it both fun and effective. I had heard of similar techniques before, such as children using worry dolls, and I see parallels with mental health strategies — speaking thoughts aloud can bring clarity and perspective. This process helps me untangle my ideas and reassess them in a more objective way

  • Reading error messages

    I am becoming more confident in reading error messages, particularly as the messages in Cursor are clear and easy to follow. Being able to identify the line and type of issue quickly has made debugging less intimidating and more approachable

  • Console.logging

    Console.logging is new to me in the way we have been using it during the course. Although I am still building confidence, it is becoming clearer each time I use it, and I can already see how valuable it is for testing assumptions and tracking what my code is actually doing

  • Googling

    Googling has been the original problem-solving tool of my generation, and I have always been confident in using it. However, I am finding that it plays a different role now — rather than being my first step, it has become a secondary resource as I develop other strategies and engage with new tools

  • Asking your peers for help

    This is an area where I am least confident. At present, I tend not to ask my peers for help, preferring to try to resolve issues on my own. I recognise that this is a growth area for me, as collaboration could provide useful perspectives and solution

  • Asking coaches for help

    I feel somewhat confident in asking coaches for help, but usually only when I have a specific problem that I cannot resolve on my own. While I value their expertise, I often prefer to attempt other strategies first.

  • Improving process with reflection

    Reflection comes naturally to me, often happening during “shower thoughts” or those moments of clarity that appear at 3am. I feel confident in this process, as it allows me to step back, evaluate what worked, and identify areas to improve in both my learning and problem-solving approach.

4. Reflect on a time you were reluctant to ask for help. Consider what made you reluctant to do so. What might you try differently next time?:

I recognise that I am most confident with self-directed problem-solving methods such as pseudocode, trying different approaches, reflecting, Googling, and reading error messages. I am also developing confidence with technical debugging tools like console.logging. However, I am least confident when it comes to collaborative methods such as asking peers or coaches for help. I often find myself reluctant to ask because I feel like I am interrupting or that I will be dismissed with unhelpful responses such as “just Google it” or having the problem repeated back to me. Unfortunately, these experiences with teachers and mentors in the past have reinforced my hesitation, making me feel that it is easier to struggle through on my own rather than seek support. If I do ask for help, it is usually because I have already exhausted all other options or because the issue feels urgent and critical. Reflecting on this, I recognise that I should not need to “validate” myself to justify asking for help. Moving forward, I would like to approach help-seeking differently by reframing it as a way to save time, gain new perspectives, and build collaborative learning, rather than as a weakness or an interruption.
